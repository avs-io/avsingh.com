I.

Now

I am an independent researcher and builder working out of New Delhi. I spent the early part of my career in venture capital at Rukam Capital, then operating roles at Noise, then back to investing. I left to build. Most of what I work on now is private and not ready for daylight.

What I will say in public:

I think the most interesting frontier is where the epistemic (how we know) meets the constructive (how we build) meets the cosmological (what the world is). I have been writing about it. I have been building toward it. The two practices are not separate.

Personal anchors, if you care for them: family first — I have a young son, a patient partner, and parents whose longevity I am actively trying to buy. The body is a system I take seriously: training, nutrition, sleep, supplementation, regular bloodwork, the boring protocol work that compounds. I read widely across cosmology, philosophy of mind, rationalism, and Indian thought. I am wary of easy answers, including the easy answers of my own tradition.

III.

A short shelf

Books I keep returning to. Not exhaustive. Not authoritative. Just the ones that did the work.

  • I Am a Strange Loop

    Douglas Hofstadter

    The self as pattern, not substance. The book I wish I had read at 19.

  • The Beginning of Infinity

    David Deutsch

    Optimism as an epistemic position, not a mood.

  • The Master and His Emissary

    Iain McGilchrist

    The divided brain, and what we lose when one hemisphere eats the other.

  • Finite and Infinite Games

    James Carse

    Two pages that have outlasted two hundred page books on strategy.

  • The Denial of Death

    Ernest Becker

    Why we build civilizations, and what we are avoiding.

  • Outlive

    Peter Attia

    The book on longevity that respects mechanism, not magic.
